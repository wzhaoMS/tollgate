# Cognitive Biases — the guardrails against ourselves

The playbook can do everything right and still fail if the operator falls into
one of these traps. Read this file before any trading session.

## 1. Survivorship bias (about Serenity)

Serenity's X account is ~10 months old. That entire window has been an AI
semiconductor super-cycle: photonics, CPO, HBM, SiC all running at the same time.

- Her method has **not** been tested in a risk-off cycle.
- Small-cap supply-chain names are the **first** to be sold in a liquidity event.
- Until her method survives at least one −20% sector drawdown, treat her
  Sharpe as an upper bound, not a base case.

Action: keep ≥ 30% cash / macro hedge until we observe her method through
a real drawdown.

## 2. Self-reported / unaudited returns

Her widely-circulated 4502% YTD is a screenshot, not an audited P&L. The
individual ticker wins are verifiable; the portfolio-weighted return is not.

Action: never anchor on her aggregate return when sizing our own positions.

## 3. Reflexivity (the Soros trap)

The more followers she gains, the more her tweets create the price moves that
"validate" her thesis. This is reflexive: success breeds success until it
breaks.

Indicators that reflexivity is breaking down:
- Tickers stop responding to her tweets
- Drawdowns of >25% appear in her named positions
- Other influencers start pre-fading her picks
- Mainstream media starts profiling her (peak attention)

When at least two of these are true, stop new entries on her current names.

## 4. Adverse selection ("why is this still cheap?")

A name remains cheap for a reason. Acceptable reasons: no coverage, too small,
foreign, niche. Unacceptable reasons: fraud, SEC investigation, auditor change,
CFO turnover, going concern doubts.

Action: every Step 0 audit must explicitly answer this question in writing.

## 5. Technical-correctness ≠ stock-correctness

A company can be technically critical to AI infrastructure and still be a bad
stock if it dilutes shareholders, runs negative gross margins, depends on a
single customer that walks, or burns cash before the revenue ramp.

Action: Step 10 (capital structure audit) is mandatory, not optional.

## 6. Story stacking

Holding three different positions that all rely on "CPO will ramp in 2027–2028"
is **one** position with three names. Theme concentration must respect the 15%
basket cap.

Action: tag each position with a `theme` field; basket caps are enforced
at the theme level.

## 7. Anchoring to her exits

She does not have exit discipline. Do not infer "she still holds, so it's
fine." She is forced to hold by position size (1% of float can take weeks to
exit). Our exit rules apply regardless of what she does.

## 8. Confirmation bias from Chinese-language community translations

Translated DD posts (e.g., 0xKevin's thread) compress nuance and emphasize wins.
Always go back to the **original English filings** before sizing.

## 9. Time-zone arbitrage illusion

Some of her wins look like clean alpha, but in fact she traded on an SEC filing
posted late US-evening that Asia hadn't priced yet by next morning. That edge is
real but small and short-lived; don't try to systematically replicate it
without automation that wakes you up.

## 10. The exit interview

Before any new position, write down on one line:
> "I will know I was wrong if ____, by ____."

If you cannot fill in both blanks, the trade is not ready.
